Fever

Fever is the most common symptom of COVID-19. Patients can experience a constant fever (< 39.1'C) that last for a few days or fluctuations in body temperature. Patients can also experience chills, which are drops in body temperature that occur before a fever. These chills can occur with shivering, which is a response of the body to generate heat.

Dry Cough

This is second most commonly observed symptom. Persistent dry cough can be indicative of pneumonia that can be caused by COVID-19 infection.

Loss of Smell and Taste

This symptom is a very strong indicator of COVID-19 infection. Research (US based) has found that loss of taste and smell is observed in 7 out of 10 patients that test positive for COVID-19. Among healthy people aged 20 to 70, loss of sense of smell and taste are a good indicator of COVID-19 infection.

Headaches

Headaches are another common symptom of COVID-19 infection. However, this symptom is associated with many other conditions and should not be used on its own to get tested. This symptom should only be concerning if other symptoms are also present.

Shortness of Breath

This symptom occurs more frequently in severe cases. Some studies have observed that this symptom is prevalent in only 8% of patients with mild symptoms.

Body Aches and Fatigue

Patient can experience tiredness and muscle pain as a result of the body's inflammatory response to the COVID-19 virus. Any fatigue and muscle pain that is occuring without cause should be monitored closely.

Diarrhoea and Other Stomach Issues

Diarrhoea, nausea, and abdominal pain are most commonly asosciated with elderly ppl (age 70 and above). Elderly ppl and those with diabetes, heart disease orobesity are more likely to show these symptoms. Stomach issues have been reported in less than 10% of patients with mild cases.

Rashes, Skin Discoloration, Chillbains

In milder infections, pink, red and purple lessions on fingers and toes, known as chillbains, have been reported across 21 countries. In some patients, this symptom emerges early during the infection. In others, this symptom occurs during recovery. For some patients, this is the only symptom that has been observed.

Predictions

Description

Forecasts for number of cumulative COVID-19 cases at the national and provincial level are determined using statistical time series models with Holt's linear trend method. The best fit model is determined by fitting models with three broad categories of trends: linear, exponential and damped exponential, where the exponent is down weighted. If the trend exhibited nationally or provincially is clearly non-linear, only exponential or damped exponential models are used. For damped exponential models, multiple models are created using dampening factors between 0.8 and 1.0, where possible.

To ensure as accurate forecasts as possible, all models are validated by removing two weeks worth of data and then forecasting for this time period. Prediction error is estimated by calculating the Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) and the Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE). The model with the smallest RMSE and MAPE error is chosen for forecasting. Forecast models are updated as more data becomes available.

Fact Checking

Susceptibility

Loss of smell and taste is a symptom of COVID-19. True

  • Along with other classical symptoms such fatigue, headache and fever, loss of smell and taste (chemosensory dysfuction) has been shown to be strongly associated with COVID-19 infection.
  • Chemosensory impairments are at least 10-fold more common in COVID-19 positive patients.
  • Chemosensory dysfunction should be used as a symptom for heightening screening of COVID-19 infections to decrease the risk of transmission.

Citation: Yan, C.H., Faraji, F., Prajapati, D.P., Boone, C.E. and DeConde, A.S. (2020), Association of chemosensory dysfunction and COVID‐19 in patients presenting with influenza‐like symptoms. Int Forum Allergy Rhinol.. Accepted Author Manuscript. doi:10.1002/alr.22579 https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/alr.22579

Smoking makes you more susceptible to COVID-19 infection. True

  • Studies have presented extensive evidence which has highlighted the negative impact of tobacco use on lung health and its causal association with respiratory diseases.
  • Specifically, in terms of COVID-19, studies have shown that smokers are 1.4 times more likely to have severe symptoms of COVID-19 and approx. 2.4 times more likely to be admitted to an ICU, need mechanical ventilation or die in comparison to non-smokers.
  • Overall, smoking is most likely associated with the negative progression of COVID-19.

Citation: Vardavas CI, Nikitara K. COVID-19 and smoking: A systematic review of the evidence. Tob Induc Dis. 2020;18:20. Published 2020 Mar 20. doi:10.18332/tid/119324 https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7083240/

Children are less susceptible to COVID-19. False

  • All individuals, including children are susceptible to SARS-CoV-2.
  • Children having immature immune system are vulnerable to the severity of the illness. Children with preexisting conditions such as heart, lung, and airway disease, malnutrition, and tumors are also at a greater risk to infection and its severity.
  • The average incubation period for children (6.5 days) is slightly longer than that of adults (5.4 days).

Citation: She, J., 2020. COVID‐19 epidemic: Disease characteristics in children. Journal of Medical Virology, 92(7).

Biological sex determines COVID-19 susceptibility. Somewhat true

  • Descriptive and observational data from Wuhan, China notes that the majority of COVID-19 affected patients have been male.
  • Earlier studies have demonstrated that sex hormones (estrogen and testosterone) impact the immune system response and engagement in different ways, resulting in a less robust immunological response in males (leading to subsequent morbidity and mortality from viral respiratory illnesses).
  • Increased ACE2 (enzyme receptors serve as a main entry point for COVID-19) activity has been found in males than females, suggesting a sex hormone influence.
  • Behavioral and cultural variables also influence current COVID-19 epidemiology; for example smoking is more prevalent amongst men in most cultures causing men to be more susceptible to infection.
  • Gender-specifc factors such as women being traditional caregivers and coordinators of care for their loved ones, particularly working mothers, result in women spending more time than men focused on medical issues related to both their own healthcare and that of their families. These factors have a significant effect on disease outcome among women vs men.

Citation: Walter, L. A. & McGregor, A. J., 2020. Sex- and Gender-specific Observations and Implications for COVID-19. West J Emerg Med, May.pp. 507-509.

Transmission

COVID-19 can be transmitted through the faeces of infected patients. Somewhat true

  • SARS-CoV RNA (genetic material) is detectable in stool samples of laboratory-confirmed COVID-19 patients. * No viral RNA was detected in urine specimens.
  • Being tested positive for SARS-CoV-2 RNA in stool specimens is not associated with the presence of gastrointestinal symptoms and the severity of illness.
  • Viral RNA continues to be detected in patients for an average of 7 days (6-10 days range) following negative pharyngeal swabs suggesting that transmission may occur even after viral clearance in respiratory tract.
  • Fecal-oral route may serve as an alternative transmission route for SARS-CoV-2.

Side Note: This study has significant impact on SOP’s concerning COVID-19 patient discharge. This, and other studies show that patients should not be discharged based purely on negative RT-PCR test results for viral RNA in pharyngeal swabs. Viral RNA detection via anal swabs/stool specimens in COVID-19 patients during hospitalization and recovery should be routinely performed and precautions must be taken until negative conversion of viral RNA in feces is seen.

Citation: Chen Y, Chen L, Deng Q, et al. The presence of SARS-CoV-2 RNA in the feces of COVID-19 patients. J Med Virol. 2020;92(7):833‐840. doi: 10.1002/jmv.25825https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32243607/

COVID-19 antibodies in breastmilk may protect infants. False

  • Breastmilk samples from laboratory confirmed COVID-19 positive patients appear to be free of SARS-CoV-2.
  • There is no evidence of the viral infection present in breastmilk.

Citation: Chen, H., 2020. Clinical characteristics and intrauterine vertical transmission potential of COVID-19 infection in nine pregnant women: a retrospective review of medical records. The Lancet, 395(10226), pp. 809-815.

There is a correlation between COVID-19 and ABO blood groups. No concrete evidence present

  • In research conducted in Wuhan and Shenzhen, O blood groups were significantly less common while blood groups were common amongst COVID-19 patients.
  • Fewer AB blood groups reported amongst COVID-19 patients.
  • An association between COVID-19 and Rh-negative blood type was found.

    • NOTE: The research on this subject is quite preliminary. Currently, there is a lack of evidence to make any conclusions. Extensive testing on larger sample sizes around the world while controlling for factors such as age, sex, and existing physical conditions are needed.

Citation: Zietz, M. and Tatonetti, N., 2020. Testing the association between blood type and COVID-19 infection, intubation, and death. medRxiv, [online] Available at: [Accessed 9 July 2020].

COVID-19 patients with the existing condition of diabetes are at increased risk of complications. True

  • COVID-19 patients with diabetes have a threefold higher mortality rate compared to patients without diabetic conditions.
  • COVID-19 patients with diabetes more likely are admitted to ICU and in need for ventilators.
  • Diabetic patients could manifest similar symptoms as patients without diabetic conditions, however, it could result in more rapid deterioration in their case.

Citation: Katulanda, P., 2020. Prevention and management of COVID-19 among patients with diabetes: an appraisal of the literature. Diabetologia, [online] pp.1440-1442. Available at: [Accessed 11 July 2020]

Treatment

Consuming lemon and other citrus fruit can prevent COVID-19 infection. Somewhat True

  • SARS-CoV-2 protease, Spike glycoprotein-RBD and PD-ACE2, are three target receptors which have been shown to contribute to virus infection.
  • Methoxy Flavonoids, found in whole fruit and greatly abundant in the peel of most Citrus , performed low binding energy to the three essential receptors.
  • Low binding energies allow these compounds (Methoxy Flavonoids) to interact tightly (ie. high binding affinity) to the target proteins and these interactions can have a potential inhibitory effect against virus infection and replication.

Citation: Utomo, R.Y.; Ikawati, M.; Meiyanto, E. Revealing the Potency of Citrus and Galangal Constituents to Halt SARS-CoV-2 Infection. Preprints 2020, 2020030214 (doi:10.20944/preprints202003.0214.v1).

Remdesivir is an effective treatment for COVID-19. Not proven as effective treatment

  • First randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled clinical trial assessing the effect of intravenous Remdesivir on patients admitted to hospital with severe COVID-19.
  • In adult patients admitted to hospital for severe COVID-19 infection, Remdesivir was not associated with statistically significant clinical benefits.
  • No statistically significant benefits were observed for Remdesivir treatment beyond those of standard care treatment.

    • Bottom line: more randomized placebo controlled clinical rails are needed before Remdesivir can be used for treatment.

Citation: Yeming Wang, et al. (2020). Remdesivir in adults with severe COVID-19: a randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled, multicentre trial, The Lancet,Volume 395, Issue 10236, Pages 1569-1578, ISSN 0140-6736. https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(20)31022-9.(http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0140673620310229)
